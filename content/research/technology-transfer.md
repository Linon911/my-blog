Technology transfer — the diffusion of knowledge, innovation, and productive capacity across firms and national borders — is one of the central mechanisms through which global economic integration translates into productivity growth and convergence. My research in this area examines how the institutional environment, particularly intellectual property protection regimes, shapes both the incentives to transfer technology and the channels through which diffusion occurs.

## Core Questions

The fundamental tension in technology transfer policy is between the static and dynamic effects of intellectual property protection. Strong IP rights may encourage knowledge disclosure and formal licensing, but they may also raise the cost of technology access for firms in developing countries. Understanding how this trade-off plays out across different institutional environments — and identifying the conditions under which IP harmonization promotes rather than restricts diffusion — is the central motivation for my empirical research.

## Empirical Strategy

My primary approach uses gravity models estimated with Poisson Pseudo-Maximum Likelihood (PPML) to analyze bilateral technology flows, exploiting variation in IP protection induced by TRIPS accession as a quasi-natural experiment. The gravity framework is well-suited to this context because it allows for zero trade flows, accounts for multilateral resistance terms, and can be extended to incorporate institutional variables at the bilateral level.
