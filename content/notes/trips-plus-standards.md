The TRIPS Agreement (1994) established a floor for intellectual property protection across WTO members. But since the mid-2000s, a growing share of preferential trade agreements (PTAs) have included so-called *TRIPS-plus* provisions that go well beyond this floor.

## What Makes a Provision TRIPS-Plus?

A provision is TRIPS-plus when it:

1. **Extends protection duration** — e.g. copyright terms beyond life+50 years, or patent extensions beyond 20 years
2. **Narrows exceptions and limitations** — restricting compulsory licensing or parallel imports
3. **Strengthens enforcement** — mandatory criminal penalties, border measures, or ISP liability

The US and EU have been the primary exporters of such standards, often via free trade agreements with smaller trading partners.

## Why This Matters for Technology Transfer

The relationship between strong IP protection and technology transfer is contested. The standard argument runs: stronger protection → greater willingness of IP holders to license → more technology flows to the host country.

The empirical record is messier. Several studies find a non-linear relationship: protection up to a threshold increases inward FDI and licensing, but beyond that threshold, strengthened rights may allow IP holders to *substitute* licensing with exports, reducing actual technology transfer.

> "The design of IP provisions in trade agreements reflects bargaining power asymmetries as much as efficiency considerations." — Maskus (2012)

## What I Am Working On

My dissertation attempts to estimate the causal effect of TRIPS-plus adoption on patent licensing flows using a staggered difference-in-differences design. The identification strategy exploits the timing of PTA entry into force across a panel of 80 countries from 1995 to 2020.

Early results suggest heterogeneous effects: TRIPS-plus adoption increases inward licensing in upper-middle-income countries but has no significant effect — and possibly a negative one — in lower-income countries.
