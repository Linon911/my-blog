A note on why broad patents may create paradoxically worse outcomes in countries with weak enforcement.

The standard argument for patent breadth is that broader protection provides stronger innovation incentives. But in developing-country contexts, where enforcement is costly and uncertain, broad patents may primarily function as barriers to entry.

## A Simple Model

Let enforcement probability be $\lambda \in [0,1]$ and patent breadth be $b \in [0,1]$. The expected value of a patent to the holder is:

$$V(b, \lambda) = b \cdot \lambda \cdot \pi_{monopoly}$$

For a technology importer, the effective access cost is $c(b, \lambda) = b \cdot (1 - \lambda) \cdot \pi_{competitive}$.

When $\lambda$ is low, increasing $b$ raises access costs without proportionally raising innovation incentives — the worst of both worlds.

## Policy Implication

TRIPS compliance on paper — adopting broad patent standards without commensurate enforcement investment — may paradoxically worsen technology access.
